Design Philosophy

How I Think About Learning

Good instructional design is invisible. The learner doesn't notice the scaffolding — they only notice that they suddenly understand something they didn't before. I build toward that moment through three interconnected principles.

01

Understand the Learner First

Before designing anything, I work to understand who is learning, why it matters to them, and where they're starting from. Instruction built without this foundation is guesswork. I use observation, feedback, and iterative refinement to stay calibrated to real learners.

02

Clarity Over Completeness

Complex material doesn't need to be delivered in full complexity. I break concepts into their smallest meaningful components, sequence them for progressive understanding, and resist the temptation to cover everything at the expense of understanding anything.

03

Learning Through Doing

Passive content consumption rarely transfers. I design for active engagement — projects, creative challenges, iterative practice. Learners should feel the material, not just encounter it. Real skill comes from making and failing and making again in a safe environment.

Designed Learning Experiences

Curriculum Design · Python · Undergraduate

CS101 — Intro to Computer Programming

A complete introductory programming course designed from scratch for first-year university students. The course deliberately eliminates traditional exams in favor of weekly hands-on assignments and a final creative project — reducing test anxiety while increasing applied skill development.

Design decision: Giving students full creative control over their final project, graded on ambition rather than perfection. This reframes the final assessment as a capstone of self-directed learning rather than a high-stakes evaluation event.
Python · Canvas LMS · Project-Based Assessment · 17-Week Sequence

Activity Design · UI/UX · Software Engineering

UI Mockup Practice — Figma Activity

A hands-on learning activity for a Software Engineering course that introduces UI design principles through direct imitation and reconstruction. Students choose a real app, recreate its interface in Figma, then reflect on their process.

Design decision: Graded entirely on effort and reflection, not accuracy or artistic skill. The instructor modeled the task publicly — showing their own TikTok recreation — removing the intimidation barrier before students began.

Program Design · Game Development · Multi-Course

Video Game Development Degree Program

Contributed to the design and launch of a new Video Game Development degree program at Southeast Missouri State University. Designed multiple courses from scratch, aligning learning outcomes with both academic standards and industry practices in game development.

Design decision: Each course was built around a project spine — students work iteratively on a single project throughout the semester, applying each new concept as it's taught rather than treating topics in isolation.

Advanced Course Design · AI · Emerging Tech

AI for Game Developers

Designed and delivered a specialized course on artificial intelligence concepts within the context of game development at Grand Canyon University. The course introduced machine learning fundamentals, pathfinding algorithms, and procedural generation through hands-on game projects.

Design decision: Grounding abstract AI concepts in immediately playable game contexts. Every concept was introduced through a working demo students could interact with before studying the underlying mechanics.

Industry Practice

Making Games, Understanding Systems

Since 2018, I've worked independently as a game developer under Kraken Inkwell. My current project, Fishy Business, is a published commercial game on Steam — an open-ended economic simulation designed around emergent player decision-making.

Building games is, at its core, an instructional design problem. Every system I design is a question: how do I present this complexity in a way that feels navigable, not overwhelming? How do I scaffold player understanding so they feel capable without feeling hand-held? How do I create feedback loops that keep people engaged?

These questions map directly onto how I think about learning design. The difference between a good game and a good course is smaller than most people think.
